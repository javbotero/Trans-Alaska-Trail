What if Alaska had a world-class “destination trail”? What if we could draw hikers, mountain bikers, and cross-country skiers to traverse tundra, taiga, temperate rainforest, and the majestic mountains and glaciers of the Brooks Range, Alaska Range, and Chugach Mountains — and cast a halo of outdoor recreation economy wherever they roam?

The poetry of the idea? This trail already exists.

The Trans-Alaska Trail — Alaska’s version of the Appalachian and Pacific Crest Trails — is an ocean-to-ocean multi-use trail spanning 800 miles across Alaska, from Arctic Ocean to Pacific tidewater, from Prudhoe Bay to Valdez. Though not well known or advertised, a gravel path already traverses Alaska from the Arctic Ocean to the Pacific.

A basic 4x4 track, or “service pad,” follows the route of the Trans-Alaska Pipeline (TAPS) and is used by Alyeska Pipeline Service Company (“Alyeska”) to main- tain the pipeline infrastructure.

The vast majority of the TAPS right-of-way is public land. Of the 800-mile right-of-way, 375 miles are federal, 344 State of Alaska, 51 Alaska Native, and 30 pri- vate. Alyeska manages right-of-way leases from Bureau of Land Management (BLM) and Alaska Department of Natural Resources (DNR). The leases do not preclude recreational use of the right-of-way. In fact, nearly the entire length of the 4x4 track is already available for recreational use on a case-by-case basis with a “Letter of Non-Objection” from Alyeska.

This is a classic “do more with what we have” idea: creatively use existing infrastructure to create new value, improve Alaskans’ quality of life, and create an an- chor attraction for Alaska’s rapidly developing adventure tourism economy.

It is important to clarify that the Trans-Alaska Trail is not a capital-“W” wilderness trail. While the TAPS right-of-way is often isolated and feels remote, the corridor always runs in proximity to a highway. The Trans-Alaska Trail idea is analogous to the wildly success- ful “rails-to-trails” corridors in the Lower 48. (Multi-use rail-trails are converted from abandoned railroad rights- of-way.)
